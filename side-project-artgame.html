Advanced Game Design — Art Game

Terms of Service Agreement

Format: Analog cooperative dungeon crawler  |  Players: 4  |  Play time: ~45 minutes

Terms of Service Agreement is a cooperative analog dungeon crawler that uses game mechanics to enact the experience of living inside a software platform. Players navigate a tile-based dungeon across three acts — but the rules change mid-game, coins get introduced without warning, ads interrupt play, and a premium tier gets offered to make it all stop. By the time the game ends, the players have agreed to everything.

The Artistic Statement

Every time you download an app, you tap "I Agree" on a document you didn't read. That document can be changed at any time. New fees can be introduced. Ads can be inserted into the experience. The company can offer you a paid tier to restore what you already had for free. And at every step, your continued use is treated as consent.

By playing this game, you have agreed to the Terms of Service.

The game doesn't explain this premise upfront. Players sit down to play what looks like a standard cooperative dungeon crawler. The artistic argument unfolds through the mechanics themselves — each System Update card is a policy change delivered mid-session, and players have no choice but to continue under the new terms or stop playing entirely.

How the Mechanics Mirror Real ToS Dark Patterns

System Updates
Rules That Change Mid-Game

Every three rooms, a new System Update card is read aloud. New rules take effect immediately. All previous rules remain in effect. Players never agreed to these rules when they sat down — but they agreed by continuing.

v1.1 — Gold Coins Introduced
Monetization Added Post-Launch

Coins don't exist at the start of the game. They're introduced in Act 1 via System Update, then become the gate for an expanding set of actions. The cost of participation increases as the game progresses.

v2.1 — Fortune System
Gambling Introduced

Act 2 introduces a gambling mechanic — spending coins for uncertain outcomes. A structural escalation of the game's economy designed to extract more from players who want to stay competitive.

v2.2 — The House
The Platform as Boss

Act 2's boss introduces a mechanic where the platform itself becomes an adversary — a mechanism that benefits The House regardless of what players do.

v3.1 — Ads Introduced
Interruption as Monetization

Beginning in Act 3, every tile flip requires players to read aloud the ad printed on the tile before play continues. The game's rhythm is broken by content no one asked for.

v3.2 — Ad-Free Premium Tier
Pay to Restore What You Had

Players may pay 5 Gold Coins to skip an ad. They are now paying to remove a friction that was added to the experience they already bought into. The premium tier is the platform selling back what it took.

The Full System Update Timeline

Version When What Gets Introduced
v1.0 Pre-game The Terms of Service Agreement — a long card read aloud before play begins. Players agree by picking up their tokens.
v1.1 Act 1 open Gold Coins introduced. Rooms now yield coins. Unspecified future costs are implied.
v1.2 Act 1 — 3rd room Background infrastructure notice. A policy change is announced. It is not optional.
v1.3 Act 1 — 6th room Referral Program. Cooperative players are rewarded for recruiting new users into the system.
v2.1 Act 2 — 3rd room Fortune System. Gambling mechanics introduced. Spend coins for uncertain outcomes.
v2.2 Act 2 — 6th room The House boss mechanic. The platform benefits regardless of player performance.
v2.3 Act 2 — 9th room Cost of Living Adjustment. Existing coin costs are revised upward. No explanation required.
v3.1 Act 3 — 3rd room Ad Cards introduced. Every tile flip is preceded by a mandatory ad read-aloud.
v3.2 Act 3 — 6th room Ad-Free Premium Tier. Players may pay 5 Gold Coins to skip an ad. The option to pay for the previous experience is now available.
v3.3 Act 3 — 9th room Everything is free. All coin costs are removed. Players win. The gesture arrives too late to matter.

Design Decisions

The dungeon crawler format was deliberate. Cooperative dungeon games are familiar, low-stakes, and friendly — exactly the kind of interface a platform wants you to feel comfortable in before it starts changing the terms. Players come in expecting to have fun together. The fun is real. The extraction is also real. That tension is the point.

Agreement slips stay on the table across all three acts. Every policy change signed off on remains visible. By Act 3, the table is covered in agreements the players made without reading.

The win condition requires completing Act 3 and hearing v3.3 — "Everything is free" — read aloud. The reward for compliance is that the platform gives back what it took. This is framed as victory. It is also the punchline.

What I Was Drawing From

This game comes directly from my background in product management and tech. I've read enough product roadmaps, monetization strategy docs, and growth playbooks to recognize the pattern: launch clean, add friction gradually, monetize the friction, offer a premium tier to remove it. The ToS is the legal infrastructure that makes all of this possible — and players click through it every day without reading a word.

Making a game about it felt like the most honest way to communicate that experience. You can explain dark patterns in a blog post. You can make someone feel them in a game.

Physical Production

Terms of Service Agreement was produced as a high-quality physical game using the Harvey Mudd Makerspace and Scripps Fab Lab. All tiles, action cards, system update cards, agreement slips, and character tokens were designed in Adobe Illustrator, printed, and hand-finished. The system update cards are sequenced and packaged so the "policy changes" are revealed in order — the physical presentation mirrors the mechanic. Playtested across multiple sessions with positive feedback.

Skills & Tools

Through the game design course and building this game, I learned and practiced:

  • Adobe Photoshop — photo editing, texture work, and visual asset production
  • 3D Printing — fabricating character tokens and game components at the Harvey Mudd Makerspace and Scripps Fab Lab
  • Graphic design for print — designing cards and tiles for physical production, accounting for bleed, resolution, and material constraints
  • Game systems design — building a rules system where each mechanic carries narrative and artistic weight, not just functional weight
  • Playtesting and iteration — running multiple playtests to tune the pacing of System Updates and the coin economy
